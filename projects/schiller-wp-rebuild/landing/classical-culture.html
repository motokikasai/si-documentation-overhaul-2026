A Schiller Institute Program · Classical Culture

Beauty ennobles.

Schiller held that it is through beauty that we become fully human — and that no republic survives without citizens so educated. This is the Institute’s oldest work.

Scroll

A people fed on ugliness will accept ugly politics; a people practiced in beauty will demand better. So we sing — in community choruses tuned to the Verdi pitch; we stage Schiller’s dramas and read his letters; we put Bach and Beethoven beside the political questions of the day, where they belong. Not culture as ornament: culture as the way a citizen’s soul is made equal to freedom.

The Idea

The aesthetic education of man

Written in the shadow of a revolution that devoured itself, Schiller’s letters argue that political freedom is impossible without the ennoblement of the individual — and that beauty is how souls are ennobled. It is the founding text of this Institute.

Read the letters, with commentary

The Practice

Choruses at the human pitch

Community choruses on three continents rehearse Bach, Mozart and Beethoven at the Verdi tuning (A = 432 Hz), sing at conferences and vigils, and prove weekly that the sublime is not a luxury — it is trainable, and it is for everyone.
