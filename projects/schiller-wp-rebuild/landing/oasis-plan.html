There will be no peace in Southwest Asia without development, and no development without water. The Oasis Plan makes water the way deserts have always been made to bloom — by building: nuclear-powered desalination on the coasts, canals connecting the Mediterranean and Red Seas to the Dead Sea, and corridors of agriculture, industry and new cities for every people of the region. Not aid — a future worth defending. That is what makes peace durable.

I

The Principle

Water is made, not fought over

The region's wars are fought over a scarcity that engineering ended decades ago in principle. A cubic meter of desalinated water costs energy — and energy, with nuclear power, is not scarce. The Oasis Plan treats fresh water as an industrial product, produced on the coasts and moved inland by canal and pipeline.

II

The Method

Development corridors, not walls

Along the canals: power plants, agro-industrial belts, and new cities — built by and for every people of the region, tied by rail into the World Land-Bridge. Where the golden spike joined a continent in 1869, the Oasis Plan joins three.

The Record

Half a century on the table

  1. 1975

    The proposal

    Lyndon LaRouche proposes nuclear-powered desalination and agro-industrial development as the basis for Middle East peace.

  2. 1990s

    The Oslo years

    The plan is circulated to governments as the economic annex peace agreements never had — development first, or the treaties die.

  3. 2024

    The revival

    As war returns to the region, the Schiller Institute convenes an international Oasis Plan conference: the alternative, fully drawn.

  4. Today

    The campaign

    Briefings, weekly organizing and diplomatic outreach on five continents — to put the plan before every government it concerns.
