The Namesake · Friedrich Schiller · 1759–1805

Why Schiller?

Why would an institute concerned with war, currency and statecraft name itself after a poet? Because Schiller answered the question every republic eventually fails: how do you make citizens strong enough for their own freedom?

Scroll · act one

Schiller watched the French Revolution — the great moment that found a little people — devour its own children, and drew the conclusion this Institute is built on: no constitution can free a population whose souls are still servile. Freedom must be rehearsed in the individual before it can be written into law, and beauty is the rehearsal. He put that argument into letters, histories, and dramas so alive that tyrants banned them on sight. We are named for him because the argument is not finished.

The Biography

A life in five acts, staged by its owner

He wrote in five acts; he lived in five acts. The rail lights each as you read.

  1. Act I · 1773

    The prisoner of the academy

    A duke conscripts the doctor’s son into his military academy: eight years, no visits, no poetry. Schiller writes his first play in secret, in the infirmary, on smuggled paper.

  2. Act II · 1782

    The fugitive

    The Robbers detonates in Mannheim; strangers embrace in the stalls. The duke forbids him to write. Schiller chooses writing, and flees — twenty-two, penniless, and suddenly the voice of everyone Europe had silenced.

  3. Act III · 1789

    The professor of history

    At Jena he opens his lectures asking: what is universal history, and why study it? His answer — that every past sacrifice is redeemed by what we build next — packs the hall beyond standing room.

  4. Act IV · 1794

    The letters after the Terror

    The Revolution fails; Schiller answers with the Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man and, with Goethe, builds Weimar into a capital of the spirit — proof that a renaissance can be organized by two stubborn friends.

  5. Act V · 1805 →

    The afterlife

    He dies at forty-five, mid-play. In 1859, on his hundredth birthday, tens of thousands march for him through New York and Berlin alike. In 1984 an institute takes his name — betting that his kind of afterlife is renewable.

One Scene

“Grant freedom of thought!”

In Don Carlos, the Marquis Posa stands before the most powerful man on Earth and asks for the one thing power cannot survive granting. Every dissident smuggling a manuscript since 1787 has been playing that scene. See it staged once and you will understand this Institute’s theory of politics better than any pamphlet could teach it.
